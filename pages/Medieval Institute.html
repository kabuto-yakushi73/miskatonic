Mission

The Medieval Institute is dedicated to the interdisciplinary study of the Middle Ages through rigorous scholarship, critical inquiry, and the preservation of cultural heritage. We advance understanding of medieval societies — their institutions, beliefs, artistic expressions, and intellectual traditions — while training the next generation of medievalists to engage with the past in ways that illuminate the present.

Vision

To be a global leader in medieval studies, fostering innovative research that bridges disciplines, geographies, and time periods. We envision an institute where scholars, students, and the public come together to explore the enduring legacy of the Middle Ages and its relevance to contemporary questions of identity, power, and cultural exchange.

Research Areas

Our faculty and students pursue innovative research across the full spectrum of medieval studies.

Medieval History & Society

Political, social, and economic history of medieval Europe from the 5th to the 15th centuries.

Medieval Literature & Philology

The study of medieval literary traditions across Latin, Old English, Old Norse, French, German, and Celtic languages.

Medieval Art & Architecture

The visual and material culture of the Middle Ages, including manuscript illumination, sculpture, and the built environment.

Medieval Philosophy & Theology

The intellectual traditions of the Middle Ages, including scholasticism, mysticism, and patristics.

Medieval Material Culture

The study of objects, artifacts, and material remains including ceramics, metalwork, and textiles.

Manuscript Studies & Codicology

The physical study of medieval manuscripts, including paleography, codicology, and textual criticism.

Medieval Music & Liturgy

The study of medieval musical traditions, including plainchant, polyphony, and liturgical practices.

Medieval Law & Governance

The study of legal systems, governance structures, and administrative practices in medieval Europe.

Medieval Environmental History

The relationship between medieval societies and their natural environments, including climate and agriculture.

Medieval Religious & Devotional Culture

The study of religious life, devotional practices, pilgrimage, and the material culture of faith.

Chamberlain Castle

The Heart of the Medieval Institute

Built in 1671 and donated to the university by Francis Chamberlain, this historic castle serves as the primary facility for the Medieval Institute. The castle combines period architecture with modern research and teaching amenities.

  • Main Area: 50 lecture rooms (converted function rooms), 50 offices, 50 bedrooms for visiting scholars and guests
  • Grand Dining Hall: A 100-seat hall for formal events, academic gatherings, and community meals
  • Kitchen: Modernized facilities supporting the dining hall and residential programs
  • Castle Basement — Level I: Chamberlain Armory (public display) and Albert Richmond Medieval Library
  • Castle Basement — Level II: Historical dungeons preserved for archaeological and historical research
  • Castle Basement — Levels III & IV: Restricted collections and catacombs accessible to qualified researchers
  • Secret Elevator: Provides access to the university's underground tunnel network

Albert Richmond Medieval Library

A Premier Research Collection

Named after its benefactor, the Albert Richmond Medieval Library houses one of the most comprehensive collections of medieval manuscripts and early printed books in North America.

  • Manuscripts: Over 25,000 medieval manuscripts spanning the 8th to 16th centuries
  • Incunabula: More than 1,200 examples of early printing from the 15th century
  • Rare Printed Works: Extensive collections of early modern editions, chronicles, and travel accounts
  • Special Collections: Focus areas include monastic chronicles, cartularies, liturgical manuscripts, and vernacular literature
  • Reading Room: A quiet, light-filled space for scholars, with access to reference works and finding aids
  • Research Carrels: Dedicated workspaces for graduate students and visiting fellows
  • Digital Resources: High-resolution digitization of selected manuscripts available through the institute's portal
Open to ResearchersDigitization Available

Castle Armory & Collections

Weapons, Armor, and Material Culture

The armory houses a significant collection of medieval and early modern weapons and armor, used for teaching, research, and public exhibitions.

  • Authentic Medieval Weapons: Swords, axes, polearms, and projectile weapons from the 12th to 16th centuries
  • Armor Collection: Full suits of plate armor, mail, helmets, and shields from various periods and regions
  • Ceremonial Objects: Maces, staffs, and decorative weapons used in academic and civic ceremonies
  • Teaching Collection: Replicas and reproductions for hands-on pedagogical use
  • Research Access: Weapons are available for scholarly examination by appointment
  • Exhibition Space: Public displays rotate throughout the year highlighting different aspects of medieval martial culture
  • Documentation: Comprehensive cataloguing including provenance, dimensions, and material analysis

Castle Catacombs & Lower Levels

Archaeology and Historical Preservation

The lower levels of Chamberlain Castle offer unique opportunities for archaeological and historical research, preserving centuries of material history.

  • Historical Dungeons: Original prison cells and holding areas preserved as they were in the 17th and 18th centuries
  • Catacombs: Extended underground passages and chambers used for storage and, historically, for burial
  • Archaeological Context: The lower levels contain stratified deposits with artifacts spanning the castle's history
  • Research Access: Controlled access for archaeological investigation and historical documentation
  • Conservation Efforts: Ongoing stabilization and environmental monitoring to preserve historic fabric
  • Interpretation: Educational programming explores the social and cultural history of the castle's lower levels
  • Connections: The catacombs connect to the university's wider tunnel network via a secure elevator
Research AccessArchaeological Site

Conservation Laboratory

Preserving the Material Past

The Conservation Laboratory is a specialized facility dedicated to the preservation, restoration, and analysis of medieval manuscripts, documents, and artifacts.

  • Manuscript Conservation: Treatment of parchment, vellum, and paper manuscripts including repair, cleaning, and binding
  • Seal and Wax Preservation: Specialized techniques for preserving medieval seals and wax impressions
  • Textile Conservation: Care and preservation of medieval textiles, tapestries, and vestments
  • Analytical Equipment: Microscopy, spectrometry, and other tools for material analysis
  • Environmental Control: Climate-controlled storage and work areas to ensure optimal preservation conditions
  • Training Program: Offers practical training for students and early-career conservators
  • Collaboration: Works with libraries, museums, and archives to advance conservation science
Training AvailableBy Appointment

Medieval Garden & Grounds

A Living History Experience

The Medieval Garden recreates the horticultural traditions of the Middle Ages, offering students and visitors a sensory engagement with the past.

  • Plant Collection: Over 200 species of plants, herbs, and flowers documented in medieval sources
  • Medicinal Herbs: A curated collection of plants used in medieval medicine and apothecary
  • Culinary Herbs: Plants used in medieval cooking, preserving, and food preparation
  • Dye Plants: Species used for textile dyeing in the medieval period
  • Ornamental Garden: Formal planting inspired by medieval monastic and aristocratic gardens
  • Teaching Resource: Used for courses in material culture, food history, and environmental history
  • Public Access: The garden is open to the public during institute hours
Open to PublicTeaching Resource

Grand Medieval Dining Hall

Tradition, Scholarship, and Fellowship

Located within Chamberlain Castle, the Grand Medieval Dining Hall serves as the social and ceremonial heart of the institute. Inspired by the great halls of medieval castles and colleges, it hosts academic banquets, conferences, receptions, and formal dinners while continuing centuries-old traditions of scholarly fellowship.

  • Capacity: Seats approximately 100 guests for formal dining and academic functions
  • Academic Banquets: Hosts graduation dinners, visiting scholars, and institute celebrations
  • Conference Venue: Used for keynote lectures, symposium receptions, and international meetings
  • Historic Design: Features timber beams, stone fireplaces, heraldic banners, and handcrafted furnishings
  • Modern Kitchen: Adjacent commercial kitchen supporting institute events and catered functions
  • Cultural Programming: Venue for medieval-themed feasts, music performances, and historical reenactments
  • Private Events: Available for university ceremonies and approved institutional gatherings

Lecture Rooms

Historic Spaces for Modern Learning

Chamberlain Castle contains fifty lecture rooms created through the adaptive reuse of its historic chambers. Each classroom preserves the architectural character of the castle while incorporating modern instructional technology for teaching and collaborative learning.

  • Teaching Spaces: 50 lecture rooms distributed throughout the castle
  • Class Sizes: Rooms accommodate seminars of 15–30 students and lectures of up to 60 participants
  • Instructional Technology: Multimedia projectors, presentation systems, wireless connectivity, and lecture capture capability
  • Seminar Rooms: Dedicated spaces designed for graduate discussions, manuscript workshops, and language instruction
  • Historic Interiors: Original stone walls, vaulted ceilings, fireplaces, and wood paneling carefully preserved
  • Flexible Layout: Movable furniture supports lectures, tutorials, workshops, and collaborative activities
  • Accessibility: Modern accessibility features integrated sensitively into the historic structure
